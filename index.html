Introduction to Enslavement

üñºÔ∏è

Media Placeholder: Ship Diagram

A plan showing how enslavers and human traffickers could fit the most enslaved humans for transport to be sold.

Dimensions: 800x600px

Slavery in America began in the early 1600s and lasted until the mid-1800s. This involved the forced labor of Black people who were captured in Africa and brought to the United States to work on plantations against their will. These enslaved Africans were treated as property with no rights or freedoms of their own, living in harsh conditions and brutal treatment at the hands of their enslavers.

Before becoming enslaved, these Africans had worked in a full range of jobs (such as skilled artisans, farmers, traders, hunters, artists, scientists, etc.) Their talents and expertise were used by white human traffickers and enslavers to make a lot of money. It's important to recognize the rich and diverse backgrounds of enslaved Africans and the impact slavery had on their lives.

enslaved African

An African forced into labor and treated as property to benefit white enslavers. Some history books call them "slaves". But slavery was something that was done to them rather than something they were.

Enslaver

A person who forces other humans to work for no pay, to obey commands, and to lose their freedom. Some history books call them "slave owners". But you can't actually own another person.

Human traffickers

People who capture and trade other humans for the purpose of enslavement or economic gain. Some history books call them "slave traders".

Fighting Against Slavery

üñºÔ∏è

Media Placeholder: Historical Print

"Horrid Massacre in Virginia" - Historical depiction of Nat Turner's rebellion

Dimensions: 700x500px

Enslaved Africans didn't just accept their situation. When they saw opportunities to escape or to fight back, they did. For example, in 1822 a group of 9,000 people, led by a formerly enslaved person named Denmark Vesey, planned to attack several South Carolina cities to revolt against slavery. However, Vesey and thirty-five other people were executed before any revolt could take place. This event led to stronger laws to control the movement and education of freedmen and slaves.

In 1831, an enslaved African from Virginia named Nat Turner set out to free all enslaved Africans in America. He and a group of his followers killed about sixty enslavers and their families across Virginia. When they were stopped, Turner and about twenty of the others were hanged. After the revolt more than 100 innocent enslaved Africans were killed by fearful enslavers. Still, for many enslaved Africans, the name Nat Turner came to represent the courage to fight oppression by whatever means necessary.

The Abolitionist Movement

üñºÔ∏è

Media Placeholder: Poster

Anti-slavery meetings poster advertising abolitionist gatherings

Dimensions: 400x500px

In the northern states, some Americans viewed slavery as shameful. They believed it had no place in modern society. The abolitionist movement began in states like New York and Massachusetts. The leaders of the movement copied some of their strategies from British activists who had turned public opinion against human trafficking and enslavement.

Abolitionists were a divided group. On one side were people like Garrison, who called for an immediate end to slavery. If that were impossible, it was thought, then the North and South should part ways. John Brown believed an armed rebellion of enslaved people in the South was the quickest route to end human bondage in the United States. On the other side were moderates who believed that slavery should be phased out gradually to make sure the Southern states would not lose money too quickly.

One of the most famous abolitionists was Harriet Tubman. She was born enslaved but escaped to freedom and risked her own life to help others escape through the Underground Railroad (a network of secret routes and safe houses).

Frederick Douglass was another influential abolitionist. He was born into slavery as well but escaped to the North where he became a powerful speaker and writer.

Many white southerners felt the abolitionists were trying to harm their way of life. They weren't wrong about that; abolitionists believed it needed to be disrupted. The white farmers and plantation owners of the South would make far less money without unpaid slave labor for their plantations and businesses. Some southern writers even tried to convince people of the ridiculous view that free Africans in the north were treated worse than enslaved Africans in the South who worked on plantations. Many white northerners didn't like the abolitionists either. They needed goods from southern businesses. They felt the abolitionists' focus on human rights caused problems for them as they sought to make money by dealing with white businesses in the south.

How Slavery Became a National Issue

Obviously, slavery was an issue the entire time for those who were enslaved. But, how did it become an issue for the country as a whole?

As of 1819, the United States had eleven slave states (a state that could practice slavery) in the South and eleven free states (states that could not practice slavery) in the North. Power in the Senate was balanced between the North and the South. Missouri, which was located in the cotton-growing region, wanted to be admitted as a slave state. The addition of Missouri as the twelfth slave state would upset the balance of power.

Media Placeholder: Historical Map

Political map showing free and slave states, circa 1820

Dimensions: 900x600px

Congress had to consider this problem before deciding whether to let Missouri enter the Union as a state that allowed slavery.

If Missouri joined the Union, it would be the first slave state west of the Mississippi River. Northerners feared slavery would spread throughout the entire Louisiana region. They thought if Missouri joined the Union as a slave state, more would follow. Congress would be unable to stop the spread of slavery.

Southern states had a different point of view. If Missouri was admitted as a free state, Congress would be able to destroy the slave system in all the states. Northerners spoke out strongly against slavery. The antislavery movement was of great concern to owners of cotton plantations in the South. The Missouri problem was beginning to heat up, as two sections of the country with strong views debated the issue. Control of the Senate by northern or southern states would determine future government policies.

How Did Maine and Missouri Become States?

The debate went on for several months. Then, by chance, Maine asked to be admitted to the Union in December of 1819. There was no question that Maine would be a free state because its constitution did not allow slavery. Maine provided a possible solution to the problem. A compromise was proposed. Missouri was to be allowed to enter the Union as a slave state. Maine would join as a free state. With twelve slave states and twelve free states, there would still be a balance of power in the Senate. In addition, slavery would not be allowed in any new states north of 36 degrees 30 minutes north latitude in the Louisiana Territory. Missouri was the only state to which this law did not apply.
